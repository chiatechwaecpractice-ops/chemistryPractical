WAEC Chemistry Practical - Test 14

Set 2 Alternative B practice: redox titration, copper(II) salt analysis, starch test and practical theory.

Specimens and Reagents

D

\(6.30\,g\,dm^{-3}\) ethanedioic acid dihydrate, \(H_2C_2O_4\cdot2H_2O\).

E

\(3.10\,g\,dm^{-3}\) potassium tetraoxomanganate(VII), \(KMnO_4\).

F

Uniform 1:1 mixture of copper(II) tetraoxosulphate(VI) and starch.

Use only the supplied reagents: NaOH, HCl, HNO3, AgNO3, Pb(NO3)2, NH3, iodine, litmus, H2SO4, zinc dust, BaCl2, methyl orange and phenolphthalein.

Teacher Class Before the Test

Teacher: This set uses acidified \(KMnO_4\) and ethanedioic acid. The endpoint is a permanent pale pink colour because \(KMnO_4\) acts as its own indicator.
\[2MnO_4^-+5C_2O_4^{2-}+16H^+\rightarrow2Mn^{2+}+10CO_2+8H_2O\]
Teacher: F contains copper(II) salt and starch. Copper(II) gives blue solution and blue precipitate tests; starch gives blue-black colour with iodine.

Enter Test 14 Question Paper

Question 1 - Quantitative Analysis

D is \(0.0500\,mol\,dm^{-3}\) ethanedioic acid. E is \(KMnO_4\) solution.

Put E in the burette. Pipette \(25.0\,cm^3\) of D into a conical flask. Add about \(10\,cm^3\) of dilute \(H_2SO_4\), warm gently and titrate with E until a permanent pale pink colour is obtained.

  1. Tabulate your burette readings.
  2. Calculate the average titre.
  3. Calculate the concentration of E in \(mol\,dm^{-3}\).
  4. Calculate the concentration of E in \(g\,dm^{-3}\). [\(KMnO_4=158\)]
  5. Calculate the percentage purity of E if the label states \(3.10\,g\,dm^{-3}\).

Question 2 - Qualitative Analysis

F is a mixture of copper(II) tetraoxosulphate(VI) and starch. Record observations and inferences.

  1. Add distilled water to F, shake and filter. Keep the filtrate and residue.
  2. Add iodine solution to a little residue.
  3. To the filtrate, add NaOH in drops and then in excess.
  4. To another portion, add NH3 in drops and then in excess.
  5. To another portion, add BaCl2 followed by dilute HCl.
  6. To another portion, add a little zinc dust and shake.

Question 3 - Practical Knowledge

  1. Why is dilute \(H_2SO_4\) added before the redox titration?
  2. Why is the solution warmed before titration?
  3. State the endpoint colour in the titration.
  4. State the confirmatory test for starch.
  5. Draw and label the titration setup and a filtration setup.

\[Average=\frac{25.50+25.45+25.50}{3}=25.48\,cm^3\]
\[n(C_2O_4^{2-})=\frac{0.0500\times25.0}{1000}=1.25\times10^{-3}\,mol\]
\[n(MnO_4^-)=\frac{2}{5}\times1.25\times10^{-3}=5.00\times10^{-4}\,mol\]
\[C_E=\frac{5.00\times10^{-4}\times1000}{25.48}=1.96\times10^{-2}\,mol\,dm^{-3}\]

Question 3 Diagram Teacher Guide

Redox Titration

Teacher: E is in the burette, D plus dilute H2SO4 is in the flask. Warm before titration and stop at permanent pale pink.

Filtration

Teacher: Residue remains on filter paper; filtrate passes into the beaker. Test both parts separately.

Copper(II) Tests

Teacher: Blue precipitate and deep blue ammonia solution are strong evidence for \(Cu^{2+}\).

  1. Dilute \(H_2SO_4\) supplies \(H^+\) needed for \(MnO_4^-\) to reduce to \(Mn^{2+}\).
  2. Warming increases the rate of reaction between \(KMnO_4\) and ethanedioic acid.
  3. Endpoint: permanent pale pink.
  4. Starch: iodine gives blue-black colouration.

WAEC Chemistry Practical — Test 11

Modelled practical question set using the supplied reagents only: H₂SO₄, NaOH, lead(II) carbonate, starch, iodine solution, HCl, HNO₃, AgNO₃, BaCl₂, NH₃, lime water, litmus papers, methyl orange and phenolphthalein.

Laboratory Reagent View

A: H₂SO₄

Dilute tetraoxosulphate(VI) acid for titration and Pb²⁺ confirmation.

B: NaOH

4.0 g dm⁻³ sodium hydroxide solution.

C: PbCO₃ + Starch

White 1:1 solid mixture for qualitative analysis.

Teacher–Student Class: Understand Before You Attempt

Teacher: In this test, WAEC can combine titration, salt analysis and practical theory. The safest path is: calculate accurately, observe carefully, and never write inference before observation.
Student: Sir, why is the titre around 12 cm³ when 25 cm³ of NaOH is used?
Teacher: Because \(H_2SO_4\) is dibasic. One mole of acid neutralizes two moles of NaOH.
\[ H_2SO_4(aq)+2NaOH(aq)\rightarrow Na_2SO_4(aq)+2H_2O(l) \]
Teacher: C contains lead(II) carbonate and starch. Water will not dissolve them properly. Iodine reveals starch; dilute nitric acid releases carbon dioxide from carbonate and converts lead carbonate into soluble lead(II) nitrate.
\[ PbCO_3(s)+2HNO_3(aq)\rightarrow Pb(NO_3)_2(aq)+CO_2(g)+H_2O(l) \]
Student: What should I write when gas is evolved?
Teacher: Write the observation fully: “Effervescence; colourless odourless gas evolved; gas turns lime water milky.” Then infer \(CO_2\) from \(CO_3^{2-}\).

Question 1 — Quantitative Analysis

A is dilute \(H_2SO_4\). B is NaOH solution containing \(4.0g\) of NaOH per \(dm^3\).

Put A into the burette. Pipette \(25.0cm^3\) of B into a conical flask. Add 2 drops of phenolphthalein and titrate with A until the pink colour just disappears. Repeat the titration to obtain concordant titre values.

  1. Tabulate your readings.
  2. Calculate the average volume of A used.
  3. Calculate the concentration of B in \(mol\,dm^{-3}\).
  4. Calculate the concentration of A in \(mol\,dm^{-3}\).
  5. Calculate the mass concentration of A in \(g\,dm^{-3}\).
  6. Calculate the number of moles of \(H^+\) in the average titre.
\[ H_2SO_4(aq)+2NaOH(aq)\rightarrow Na_2SO_4(aq)+2H_2O(l) \]

[H = 1, O = 16, S = 32, Na = 23]

Question 2 — Qualitative Analysis

C is a mixture of lead(II) carbonate and starch. Carry out the following tests. Record your observations and state your inferences.

  1. Add distilled water to C and shake. Test with red and blue litmus papers.
  2. Filter the mixture. Keep the residue and filtrate.
  3. Add iodine solution to a small portion of the residue.
  4. Add dilute \(HNO_3\) to another portion of the residue and warm gently.
  5. Pass any gas evolved into lime water.
  6. Divide the resulting acid solution into six portions.
  7. To the first portion, add NaOH(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  8. To the second portion, add NH₃(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  9. To the third portion, add dilute H₂SO₄(aq).
  10. To the fourth portion, add dilute HCl(aq).
  11. To the fifth portion, add BaCl₂(aq), then excess dilute HNO₃.
  12. To the sixth portion, add AgNO₃(aq), then excess dilute HNO₃.

Question 1 Solution

Titration Rough 1st 2nd 3rd
Final burette reading / cm³ 12.40 12.15 12.10 12.15
Initial burette reading / cm³ 0.00 0.00 0.00 0.00
Volume of A used / cm³ 12.40 12.15 12.10 12.15
\[ Average\ titre=\frac{12.15+12.10+12.15}{3}=12.13cm^3 \]

Concentration of B

\[ Molar\ mass\ of\ NaOH=23+16+1=40g\,mol^{-1} \] \[ C_B=\frac{4.0}{40}=0.100mol\,dm^{-3} \]

Concentration of A

\[ H_2SO_4:NaOH=1:2 \] \[ \frac{C_A V_A}{C_B V_B}=\frac{1}{2} \] \[ \frac{C_A\times12.13}{0.100\times25.0}=\frac{1}{2} \] \[ C_A=\frac{0.100\times25.0}{2\times12.13}=0.103mol\,dm^{-3} \]

Mass concentration of A

\[ Molar\ mass\ of\ H_2SO_4=98g\,mol^{-1} \] \[ Mass\ concentration=0.103\times98=10.1g\,dm^{-3} \]

Moles of \(H^+\)

\[ n(H_2SO_4)=\frac{0.103\times12.13}{1000}=1.25\times10^{-3}mol \] \[ n(H^+)=2(1.25\times10^{-3})=2.50\times10^{-3}mol \]

Question 2 Solution

TestObservationInference
C + water + litmus White suspension; no change on red or blue litmus. Insoluble neutral/near-neutral mixture.
Filtration Colourless filtrate and white residue. Insoluble component present.
Residue + iodine Blue-black colouration. Starch present.
Residue + dilute HNO₃ + warm Effervescence; colourless odourless gas evolved. Carbonate suspected; CO₂ evolved.
Gas + lime water Lime water turns milky. CO₂ confirmed; CO₃²⁻ present.
Acid solution + NaOH in drops White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺, Al³⁺ or Zn²⁺ may be present.
Then excess NaOH White precipitate dissolves. Amphoteric ion present.
Acid solution + NH₃ in drops White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺, Al³⁺ or Zn²⁺ may be present.
Then excess NH₃ White precipitate remains insoluble. Pb²⁺ indicated.
Acid solution + dilute H₂SO₄ White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺ confirmed as PbSO₄.
Acid solution + dilute HCl White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺ confirmed as PbCl₂.
Acid solution + BaCl₂ + dilute HNO₃ No white precipitate. SO₄²⁻ absent.
Acid solution + AgNO₃ + dilute HNO₃ No white precipitate. Cl⁻ absent.

Question 3 Solution Guide

Titration Setup

Label burette, clamp, stand, conical flask, white tile, acid A, alkali B, and indicator.

Teacher: This diagram explains how Question 1 is carried out. The burette must be vertical and the flask must be swirled during titration.

Filtration Setup

Label funnel, filter paper, residue, filtrate, and receiving beaker or conical flask.

Teacher: Filtration separates the insoluble part of C from the liquid. In this test, the white residue is important for further testing.

CO2 Lime Water Test

Label reaction tube, delivery tube, lime water, bubbles, and gas path.

Teacher: When carbonate reacts with acid, carbon dioxide is released. Lime water turning milky confirms the gas.

Burette Meniscus

For 12.10 cm3, the reading line must touch the bottom of the meniscus.

Teacher: Eye level prevents parallax error. Always write burette readings with two decimal places.

  1. Phenolphthalein: colourless in acid, pink in alkali.
  2. Methyl orange: red in acid, yellow in alkali, orange at end point.
  3. Dilute HNO₃ is preferred because it does not introduce Cl⁻ or SO₄²⁻.

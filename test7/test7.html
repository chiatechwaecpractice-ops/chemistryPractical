WAEC Chemistry Practical — Test 6B

Alternative school-practice version using the same reagents: H₂SO₄, NaOH, lead(II) carbonate and starch.

Candidate’s Instruction

Answer all questions. Record all observations accurately and give inferences only after stating the observation. Show all calculation steps clearly.

Reagents:
A = dilute tetraoxosulphate(VI) acid, H₂SO₄
B = sodium hydroxide solution containing 4.0 g dm⁻³ NaOH
C = mixture of lead(II) carbonate and starch

Enter Class: Question Paper

Question 1 — Quantitative Analysis

A is dilute H₂SO₄. B is NaOH solution containing 4.0 g of NaOH per dm³.

Put A into the burette. Pipette 20.0 cm³ of B into a conical flask. Add two drops of phenolphthalein and titrate with A until the pink colour just disappears. Repeat to obtain concordant titres.

  1. Tabulate your burette readings.
  2. Calculate the average volume of A used.
  3. Calculate the concentration of B in mol dm⁻³.
  4. Calculate the concentration of A in mol dm⁻³.
  5. Calculate the number of moles of H⁺ ions in the average titre of A.
\[ H_2SO_4(aq)+2NaOH(aq)\rightarrow Na_2SO_4(aq)+2H_2O(l) \]

[H = 1, O = 16, S = 32, Na = 23]

Label the burette, retort stand, clamp, conical flask, white tile and indicator.

Question 2 — Qualitative Analysis

C is a mixture of lead(II) carbonate and starch. Carry out the following tests. Record your observations and state the conclusions drawn.

  1. Add distilled water to C, shake well and filter. Keep the residue.
  2. Add iodine solution to a small portion of the residue.
  3. Add dilute HNO₃ to another portion of the residue and warm gently.
  4. Pass any gas evolved into lime water.
  5. Divide the acid solution obtained into three portions.
  6. To the first portion, add NaOH(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  7. To the second portion, add NH₃(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  8. To the third portion, add KI(aq), then warm gently.

Question 3 — Practical Knowledge

  1. Why is phenolphthalein colourless at the end point in this titration?
  2. Why should the pipette be rinsed with B before use?
  3. State one chemical test for starch.
  4. State one confirmatory test for Pb²⁺.
  5. What observation confirms CO₂?
  6. Why should lead compounds not be disposed carelessly into the environment?

Question 1 Solution

Titration Rough 1st 2nd 3rd
Final reading / cm³ 10.05 9.75 9.80 9.75
Initial reading / cm³ 0.00 0.00 0.00 0.00
Volume of A used / cm³ 10.05 9.75 9.80 9.75
\[ Average\ titre=\frac{9.75+9.80+9.75}{3}=9.77cm^3 \]

Concentration of B

\[ Molar\ mass\ of\ NaOH=23+16+1=40g mol^{-1} \] \[ C_B=\frac{4.0}{40}=0.100mol\,dm^{-3} \]

Concentration of A

\[ H_2SO_4:NaOH=1:2 \] \[ \frac{C_A V_A}{C_B V_B}=\frac{1}{2} \] \[ \frac{C_A\times9.77}{0.100\times20.0}=\frac{1}{2} \] \[ C_A=\frac{0.100\times20.0}{2\times9.77}=0.102mol\,dm^{-3} \]

Moles of H⁺ ions in average titre

Since 1 mole of H₂SO₄ gives 2 moles of H⁺:

\[ n(H_2SO_4)=\frac{0.102\times9.77}{1000}=9.97\times10^{-4}mol \] \[ n(H^+)=2\times9.97\times10^{-4} =1.99\times10^{-3}mol \]
The titre is realistic because 20.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ NaOH requires about 10.0 cm³ of approximately 0.100 mol dm⁻³ H₂SO₄ due to the 1:2 mole ratio.

Question 2 Solution

Test Observation Inference
C + distilled water, shake and filter White suspension formed; solid remains as residue. C contains insoluble substances.
Residue + iodine solution Blue-black colouration formed. Starch is present.
Residue + dilute HNO₃ and warm Effervescence; colourless odourless gas evolved. Carbonate suspected.
Gas + lime water Lime water turns milky. CO₂ confirmed; CO₃²⁻ present.
Acid solution + NaOH in drops White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺, Zn²⁺ or Al³⁺ may be present.
Then excess NaOH White precipitate dissolves. Amphoteric ion present.
Acid solution + NH₃ in drops White precipitate formed. Pb²⁺, Al³⁺ or Zn²⁺ may be present.
Then excess NH₃ White precipitate remains insoluble. Pb²⁺ indicated.
Acid solution + KI(aq), warm Yellow precipitate formed; dissolves on heating and recrystallizes as golden-yellow crystals on cooling. Pb²⁺ confirmed as PbI₂.

Equations

\[ PbCO_3(s)+2HNO_3(aq)\rightarrow Pb(NO_3)_2(aq)+CO_2(g)+H_2O(l) \]
\[ CO_2(g)+Ca(OH)_2(aq)\rightarrow CaCO_3(s)+H_2O(l) \]
\[ Pb^{2+}(aq)+2OH^-(aq)\rightarrow Pb(OH)_2(s) \] \[ Pb(OH)_2(s)+2OH^-(aq)\rightarrow [Pb(OH)_4]^{2-}(aq) \]
\[ Pb^{2+}(aq)+2I^-(aq)\rightarrow PbI_2(s) \]

Question 3 Solution

Titration Setup

Teacher: The white tile makes the endpoint easier to see during titration.

Pb2+ Confirmation

Teacher: Use the flowchart to connect white precipitates with lead(II) ion confirmation.

  1. Phenolphthalein becomes colourless when the alkali has been neutralized by acid.
  2. The pipette is rinsed with B to avoid dilution and contamination of the measured NaOH.
  3. Add iodine solution; blue-black colour confirms starch.
  4. Add KI; yellow PbI₂ precipitate confirms Pb²⁺. Dilute H₂SO₄ also gives white PbSO₄.
  5. CO₂ turns lime water milky.
  6. Lead compounds are poisonous and can pollute soil and water.

Exam-Ready Takeaway

Best Titre Skill

Use only concordant titres for average. Ignore rough titre.

Best Qualitative Skill

Write observation before inference.

Pb²⁺ Confirmation

Yellow PbI₂ with KI or white PbSO₄ with dilute H₂SO₄.

Starch

Iodine solution gives blue-black colouration.

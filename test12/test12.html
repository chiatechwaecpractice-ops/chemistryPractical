Final Reagent and Equipment Summary

A: Dilute H₂SO₄

Strong dibasic acid. Used in titration against NaOH and also forms white PbSO₄ with Pb²⁺.

\[H_2SO_4 \rightarrow 2H^+ + SO_4^{2-}\]

B: NaOH Solution

Contains \(4.0g\,dm^{-3}\) NaOH. It is a strong alkali used in titration and cation testing.

\[C=\frac{4.0}{40}=0.100mol\,dm^{-3}\]

C: PbCO₃ + Starch

White mixture. PbCO₃ gives CO₂ with acid. Starch gives blue-black colour with iodine.

HNO₃

Best acid for dissolving PbCO₃ before cation tests because nitrate salts are generally soluble.

NaOH and NH₃

Used to test cations. Pb²⁺ gives white precipitate with both; dissolves in excess NaOH but not in excess NH₃.

BaCl₂ and AgNO₃

BaCl₂ tests for sulphate. AgNO₃ tests for chloride. They are included to test students’ reasoning.

Class Section: The Lab of Explanations

Teacher: Candidate, this final test is designed like real WAEC practical. You must behave like a practical chemist: measure, observe, infer and explain.
Student: Sir, what should I do first in titration?
Teacher: Rinse the burette with A and the pipette with B. Fill the burette with A. Pipette B into the conical flask, add indicator, then titrate carefully until the endpoint appears.
Accuracy strategy: Near the endpoint, add A dropwise, swirl continuously, and wash down the sides of the flask with distilled water if droplets stick to the wall.
Student: Why is the expected titre not 25 cm³?
Teacher: Because \(H_2SO_4\) is dibasic. \(1\) mole of \(H_2SO_4\) neutralizes \(2\) moles of NaOH. So the acid volume is expected to be about half of the alkali volume when their molarities are close.
\[ H_2SO_4 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SO_4 + 2H_2O \]
Student: How do I handle C?
Teacher: C is a solid mixture. Water gives a suspension. Iodine checks starch. Acid checks carbonate. The acid solution formed after carbonate reacts gives \(Pb^{2+}\) for cation tests.
WAEC trap: Do not write “CO₂” as the observation. Write: “Effervescence; colourless odourless gas evolved; lime water turns milky.”
Teacher: BaCl₂ and AgNO₃ are not wasted reagents. They teach you to identify negative results. In this specimen, sulphate and chloride are not the original anions, so BaCl₂ and AgNO₃ should not give the positive results expected for sulphate or chloride.

Question 1 — Quantitative Analysis

A is dilute tetraoxosulphate(VI) acid, \(H_2SO_4\). B is sodium hydroxide solution containing \(4.0g\) of NaOH per \(dm^3\).

Put A into the burette. Pipette \(25.0cm^3\) of B into a conical flask. Add 2 drops of phenolphthalein and titrate with A until the pink colour just disappears. Repeat the titration to obtain concordant titres.

  1. Tabulate your readings.
  2. Calculate the average titre.
  3. Calculate the concentration of B in \(mol\,dm^{-3}\).
  4. Calculate the concentration of A in \(mol\,dm^{-3}\).
  5. Calculate the concentration of A in \(g\,dm^{-3}\).
  6. Calculate the number of moles of \(H^+\) in the average titre.
  7. Calculate the volume of A needed for \(20.0cm^3\) of B.
  8. State the endpoint colour change with phenolphthalein.
  9. If methyl orange were used, state the expected colour change.

Question 2 — Qualitative Analysis

C is a uniform mixture of lead(II) carbonate and starch. Carry out the following tests. Record observations and inferences.

  1. Add distilled water to C and shake. Test with red and blue litmus papers.
  2. Filter the mixture and keep both residue and filtrate.
  3. Add iodine solution to a portion of the residue.
  4. Add dilute \(HNO_3\) to another portion of the residue and warm gently.
  5. Pass any gas evolved into lime water.
  6. Divide the resulting acid solution into six portions.
  7. To portion I, add NaOH(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  8. To portion II, add NH₃(aq) in drops and then in excess.
  9. To portion III, add dilute H₂SO₄(aq).
  10. To portion IV, add dilute HCl(aq).
  11. To portion V, add BaCl₂(aq), followed by excess dilute HNO₃.
  12. To portion VI, add AgNO₃(aq), followed by excess dilute HNO₃.

Question 3 Solution Guide

Titration Setup

Required labels: burette, clamp, retort stand, acid A, conical flask, B, indicator, and white tile.

Teacher: Use this to explain the whole titration process: measure B with a pipette, add indicator, then run A from the burette to the endpoint.

Filtration Setup

Required labels: filter funnel, filter paper, residue, filtrate, and receiving beaker.

Teacher: The residue is tested further because it contains the insoluble part of the mixture. The filtrate is the liquid collected below.

CO2 Gas Test

Required labels: test tube with C and HNO3, delivery tube, lime water, and gas bubbles.

Teacher: The gas must enter the lime water through the delivery tube. Milky lime water confirms CO2 from carbonate.

Burette Meniscus

Required labels: eye level, bottom of meniscus, and reading mark.

Teacher: The meniscus must be read from the bottom at eye level. This is how students avoid parallax error.

Accuracy Strategies

  1. Read burette at eye level.
  2. Record readings to 2 decimal places.
  3. Do not average the rough titre.
  4. Use only concordant titres.
  5. Add acid dropwise near endpoint.
  6. Swirl the flask continuously.

Errors to Avoid

  1. Using wrong mole ratio.
  2. Writing inference as observation.
  3. Failing to warm gently where gas evolution is slow.
  4. Using HCl before AgNO₃ chloride test.
  5. Forgetting units in calculations.
  6. Wrongly averaging rough titre.

Final WAEC secret: Observation is what happens. Inference is what it means. Example: “Lime water turns milky” is observation; “CO₂ is present” is inference.

Round Summary of Key Facts

Titration

\(H_2SO_4:NaOH = 1:2\). Expected titre for 25.0 cm³ B is about 12.1 cm³.

Endpoint

Phenolphthalein: pink to colourless. Methyl orange: yellow to orange/pink.

Carbonate

Acid gives effervescence. CO₂ turns lime water milky.

Starch

Iodine solution gives blue-black colouration.

Pb²⁺

White ppt with NaOH, soluble in excess NaOH; white ppt with NH₃, insoluble in excess.

Confirm Pb²⁺

White PbSO₄ with H₂SO₄ or white PbCl₂ with HCl.

BaCl₂

Checks sulphate. Positive result is white BaSO₄ insoluble in dilute HNO₃.

AgNO₃

Checks chloride. Positive result is white AgCl precipitate.
